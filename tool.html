Full strategy guide — how it works and why (for beginners)

The big idea

This strategy is dollar-cost averaging (DCA) with a volume knob. Plain DCA means buying a fixed amount every week no matter what — a great habit, because it removes emotion and timing. This tool keeps that habit but turns the knob: buy more when Bitcoin is historically cheap, less (or nothing) when it is historically expensive. It never predicts the future. It only measures where today's price sits relative to Bitcoin's own long-term averages, and reacts. You decide on one fixed base amount (say €100/week); the tool tells you each week whether to invest 0×, 0.5×, 1×, 1.5×, 2× or 3× that amount.

The four measuring sticks

1. The 200-week average ("the floor"). The average price of the last ~4 years — one full market cycle. Because it moves so slowly, it acts as Bitcoin's long-term fair-value floor: every bear market since 2015 has bottomed almost exactly on it. When today's price touches this line, history says it's the best buying opportunity a cycle offers. That's why it triggers the maximum 3×.

2. The Mayer Multiple ("the thermometer"). Today's price divided by the 200-day average. It answers: how far has price strayed from its own recent normal? Around 1.0 = normal. Below 0.8 = price is 20%+ below normal (historically cheap → buy 2×). Above 1.40 = stretched (slow down → 0.5×). Above 1.65 = very stretched (stop buying → 0×). Above 2.40 = mania (see selling, below). One instrument, read at different temperatures.

3. The Bull Market Support Band ("the trampoline"). A band made of two ~5-month averages. During healthy bull markets, price repeatedly dips to this band and bounces off it. A dip below the band while the thermometer is still moderate is usually a discount inside an uptrend — worth buying at 1.5×.

4. The Pi Cycle ratio ("the mania detector"). A ratio of a fast average to a slow one. When the fast one overtakes 2× the slow one, price has risen absurdly fast — this called the manic tops of 2013, 2017 and 2021 within days. It only matters for selling, never for buying.

How the buying rules relate to each other

The six rules are not steps on one scale — they are different alarms watching different things, arranged by priority. The floor (3×) outranks the thermometer (2×), which outranks the trampoline (1.5×). The overheat readings (0.5×, 0×) come from the thermometer's hot end and can never be true at the same time as its cold end. In deep bear markets several buy alarms ring at once — price sits on the floor AND is cheap AND is under the band. That's normal and the dashboard marks the losers "also true, but outranked": the strategy simply acts on the strongest signal. In quiet times no alarm rings and you buy the normal 1×. Historically that's the single most common week — about 43% of the last four years.

Selling: two rare "mania insurance" rules

Backtesting showed that frequent profit-taking destroys value for a long-term accumulator — every sell level low enough to catch modern, calmer market tops also fires during ordinary rallies, selling coins that should have been kept. So this strategy sells in exactly two situations, both extreme: Mayer above 2.40, or the Pi Cycle lines crossing. Each triggers a sale of just 10% of holdings, once per cycle (they re-arm only after the market has fully cooled down, Mayer < 0.8). These levels were reached in 2013, 2017 and 2021 — but not in the 2025 cycle, and as Bitcoin matures they may never be reached again. That's fine: the rules then simply never fire and cost nothing. They exist as insurance in case Bitcoin has one more mania in it.

If a sale does happen, the cash isn't profit to spend — it's a reserve waiting to buy back cheaper: deploy 25% of it per week whenever price is at the floor, or 10% per week while Mayer < 0.8.

What to expect in practice

Most weeks are boring — that's by design. The strategy earns its edge in the uncomfortable moments: it demands 3× your base amount for months on end during bear markets, exactly when the news is worst (in 2022–23 the 3× zone lasted over half a year). Choose a base amount where 3× is comfortably affordable — an abandoned plan is worse than a modest one. Expect brutal interim drawdowns: in the backtest the portfolio fell ~78% from its peak before recovering; holding through that is the price of the returns. Over 2014–2026 this ruleset accumulated roughly 60% more Bitcoin per euro invested than plain DCA — but that rests on only four market cycles of history, and no rule based on the past is guaranteed a future. This is a decision aid, not financial advice.

Two completely different cycles, the same per-dollar edge (+26%), with zero tuning between them. Notes for honesty: the strategy deliberately invests more than plain DCA — mostly during bear markets, so it requires spare cash exactly when markets feel worst; and two cycles is a small sample. Past results never guarantee future ones.
